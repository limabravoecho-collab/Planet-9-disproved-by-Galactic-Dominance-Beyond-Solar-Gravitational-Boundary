## APPENDIX A: MATHEMATICAL DERIVATIONS

### DERIVATION 1: HILL SPHERE RADIUS

Consider a three-body system: primary mass M (Galaxy), secondary mass m (Sun), test particle mass μ (Oort Cloud object, μ << m).

In the rotating frame centered on m, the effective potential is:

Φ_eff = -GM/|r_M| - Gm/|r_m| - (1/2)ω² r²

Where:
- r_M = position relative to M
- r_m = position relative to m
- ω = orbital angular velocity of m around M
- r = distance from rotation axis

The Hill sphere boundary is where the Jacobi constant allows escape from m's influence. Approximating for r_m << a (orbital radius of m around M):

The tidal force from M at distance r from m in the direction toward/away from M:

F_tidal ≈ (2GMr) / a³

Setting equal to gravitational force from m:

Gm / r² = (2GMr) / a³

Solving for r:

r³ = (m/2M) a³

r = a (m/2M)^(1/3)

The factor of 2 vs 3 in denominator depends on exact definition (L1 point vs average Hill radius). Standard Hill radius uses factor of 3:

r_H = a (m/3M)^(1/3)

This is the formula used in calculations above.

### DERIVATION 2: JACOBI RADIUS FOR TIDAL STRIPPING

The Jacobi radius (also called Roche radius in some contexts) represents where external tidal forces overcome internal binding.

For object at distance r from Sun, experiencing galactic tidal field with 
gradient dF/dr:

External tidal acceleration: a_ext = (dF/dr) × r

Internal gravitational acceleration: a_int = GM_☉ / r²

Setting equal for critical radius:

(dF/dr) × r = GM_☉ / r²

r³ = GM_☉ / (dF/dr)

For galactic tidal field at solar location:

dF/dr ≈ 4πGρ_gal

Where ρ_gal is local galactic density (Poisson equation for spherically symmetric mass distribution).

Therefore:

r_Jacobi = (GM_☉ / 4πGρ_gal)^(1/3)

r_Jacobi = (M_☉ / 4πρ_gal)^(1/3)

This is the formula used for Jacobi radius calculation above.

Note: For elliptical orbits, the effective stripping radius depends on eccentricity. Objects with high eccentricity (e ~ 0.9-0.99, typical for Oort Cloud) have aphelion at much larger distances, making them vulnerable to stripping even if semi-major axis is inside Jacobi radius.

### DERIVATION 3: CUMULATIVE PERTURBATION FROM DIFFERENTIAL ROTATION

An object at distance d from Sun (perpendicular to galactic center direction) experiences different orbital velocity than Sun due to galactic shear.

Velocity difference:

Δv = v(R_☉ + d) - v(R_☉) ≈ (dv/dR) × d

Using Oort's constant A:

A = (1/2)(v/R - dv/dR)

Therefore:

dv/dR = v/R - 2A

For small d << R:

Δv ≈ 2A × d

This velocity difference accumulates as a secular perturbation. For object on orbit with period P, each orbit induces phase shift:

Δφ = (Δv / v_orbit) × 2π

Over N orbits, this accumulates as random walk (since direction of perturbation varies with orbital phase):

Δv_cumulative = Δv × √N

Where N = t_total / P

This is the basis for differential rotation perturbation calculation above.

### DERIVATION 4: STELLAR ENCOUNTER PERTURBATION

When star of mass M_* passes at distance b with relative velocity v_rel, the impulse imparted to object at distance r from Sun is approximately:

Δv ≈ (2GM_*) / (b × v_rel)

More precisely, for hyperbolic encounter with impact parameter b:

Δv = (2GM_*) / (b × v_rel) × f(r, b, geometry)

Where f is a geometric factor depending on object's position relative to encounter trajectory.

For object in outer Oort Cloud (r >> b typically):

f ≈ (r/b) for r perpendicular to encounter trajectory
f ≈ 1 for r along encounter trajectory

Typical values for solar neighborhood:
- M_* ~ 0.5 M_☉
- b ~ 0.3-1 ly (10^4-10^5 AU)
- v_rel ~ 30 km/s

Gives Δv ~ 10-100 m/s per encounter, consistent with numerical simulations.

### DERIVATION 5: ESCAPE VELOCITY DECLINE

Solar escape velocity at distance r:

v_esc(r) = √(2GM_☉/r)

At different distances:

r = 1,000 AU:    v_esc = 4,210 m/s
r = 10,000 AU:   v_esc = 1,331 m/s
r = 100,000 AU:  v_esc = 421 m/s
r = 200,000 AU:  v_esc = 298 m/s
r = 1 ly:        v_esc = 133 m/s

Note: v_esc ∝ r^(-1/2)

This declining escape velocity makes distant objects increasingly vulnerable to perturbations of fixed amplitude. A 50 m/s perturbation that is negligible at 1,000 AU (1% of v_esc) becomes significant at 200,000 AU (17% of v_esc).

Multiple perturbations of this magnitude can unbind the object through random walk accumulation.
